Where we come from
Origins
根源 · 隴西李氏 · 台山

Our family — the Lees — belongs to the Longxi Li lineage (隴西李氏) — the northwestern noble clan that founded the Tang Dynasty, produced China's greatest emperors, and counted the poet Li Bai among its members. Our branch arrived in Taishan in 1102–1103 CE when 聯鑑翁 (Lianjian Weng, born 1036 CE) sailed south by imperial commission, encountered a typhoon, and died pointing toward a mountain to show where the family should settle. The genealogy spans exactly 982 years from his birth to its 2018 publication.聯鑑翁受皇命鎮撫粵邊，率船南下，途中遭颶風，舟師多病。翁力疾而終，臨終以手指山，示後人定居之所。此山即吾族台山發祥之地。

吾族屬隴西李氏，乃西北望族，創建大唐王朝，誕生中國最偉大之帝王，詩仙李白亦出於同宗。

The source documents

Four documents combined: the Kingston family tree · the 台山李氏族譜 (Taishan clan genealogy, 2018) · Lee Yick Poon's 1963 diary translated by granddaughter Lillian Wai Yin Lee Lui · and a family email thread initiated by Olivia Lee in 2015. The birth year 1192 CE for ancestor 金山 (Jin Shan) appears in both the family tree and the Taishan genealogy — locking the two documents together.

Taishan · 台山 · 第一僑鄉

Taishan calls itself "The First Home of Overseas Chinese." An estimated half a million Chinese Americans are of Taishanese descent. Before 1965, nearly all Chinese in North American Chinatowns spoke Taishanese (台山話) — our family's dialect. Our branch is the 【巨江房】 line, founded by 莊泉 (Zhong Chuan), our confirmed 太始祖.

Olivia's research

Olivia Lee — Asian American Studies degree, immigration law background, friend of historian Him Mark Lai — initiated this project in 2015. She accessed immigration records and gathered memories from aunts, uncles, and cousins. Aunt Lillian Lui translated Lee Yick Poon's diary and prepared the family tree that made it possible.

Confirmed records

Lee Yick Poon (Poo Lee): 4 US records confirmed — 1942 military draft, 1959 naturalization petition, 1965 naturalization certificate, arrival record. Yau Jee: 3 US records confirmed — 1910 census, 1897 ship arrival, 1941 death record (Mt. Auburn Cemetery, Chicago).

Early ancestors · from the 1963 memoir
19th century · Gen. 20
Siu Yip — silver found twice
First of our line to settle in Hoy Yin District. While reburying his father's tomb he found silver coins in a brook — opened a duck farm. Later, while widening a road to help the public, he uncovered a second coffin full of silver coins and bought rice fields yielding 3,000–4,000 dom. Two acts of charity, twice rewarded by fortune hidden in the earth.
19th century · Gen. 23
Sing Jom — the miser who saved the family
Owned a farming business, rice and wine business, and pig farm. "He was so miserly that he ate one tiny fish with three mouthfuls of rice." 先輩承淦公，勤儉持家，據族叔耀興所言，其節儉至極，每餐僅以一小魚佐三口飯。然正因如此，方能積蓄田產，計年收一百五十石，分授三子。 This thriftiness let him buy rice fields yielding 150 dom, passed to three sons. The eldest and third sons were lazy and lost their land. Only our ancestor — his second son Tin Wai — retained his fields.
19th century · Gen. 24
Tin Wai — educator in the South Seas
Went to the South Seas as an educator and a doctor — but earned only enough for self-subsistence and sent just $2 Chinese home once a year, with a letter saying he was well. The family survived on that letter and that $2, year after year.
1897–1941 · Gen. 25 · Chicago
Yau Jee — arrived 1897 on the SS Gaelic
Lee Yick Poon's father arrived in San Francisco on the SS Gaelic, 27 November 1897, age 28, with Chicago already listed as his last residence — suggesting he had connections there before this arrival. The 1910 census records his immigration year as 1887, but this is almost certainly a transcription error: Yau Jee needed to be in China for Lee Yick Poon to be born in 1891, and the memoir describes him farming at home through that period. The 1897 ship record is the primary document and is almost certainly his true first arrival. He settled in Chicago and worked as a laundryman on Kensington Avenue, Ward 33. He won a lottery and sent $3,000 Chinese home. When Lee Yick Poon arrived in Chicago in 1929, his father was still there — father and son spent twelve years in the same city. Yau Jee died on 8 April 1941, age 73, and was buried at Mt. Auburn Cemetery, Cook County, Illinois. His wife's name is recorded in the death record as Neonee — Lee Yick Poon's mother, who had died in China in 1917.

Lee Yick Poon · Diary · March 1963 · 李奕揖 · 一九六三年三月日記
"In 1929, I posed as a sailor on a freight container to come over to the United States. First I worked in Yip Dong's restaurant [in Chicago] earning a monthly salary of $20. Later it was increased to $60 but dropped again to $20 as the business declined. Eventually, the restaurant shut down. Yip Dong helped me repay the debt of over $2,000. The hardship I endured during that period was indescribable."
「一九二九年，余喬裝水手，藏身貨船，渡海抵美。彼時世界經濟蕭條，百業凋零。初在葉東兄餐館任職，月薪僅二十元。其間艱辛困苦，難以言喻。」
Lee Yick Poon · Diary · March 1963 · 李奕揖 · 一九六三年三月日記
"Even though I did not have outstanding and notable achievements, I have raised my children and they have established their own families. My goal has been fulfilled. Today I returned to Hong Kong to visit my sons and grandchildren and I feel extremely comforted at seeing them. My only regret is that my wife left me so early in life."
「余雖無顯赫功業，然育有子女，各自成家立室，夙願已了。今日歸港，與子孫相聚，深感欣慰。平生唯一憾事，乃內子早逝，未能共享晚年之樂。」

In America he went by Poo Lee — his name reversed. He used the Chinese Confession Program (1956–1965) to legalize his undocumented 1929 entry, and became a US citizen on 17 February 1965, age 73, at the District Court in San Francisco. His naturalization certificate lists his address as 4 Spofford Alley, San Francisco — confirmed on record.

He was always ranked 1st or 2nd in school and became a teacher at 23. In June 1923 his village was burned by thieves and the family fled as nomads to Hong Kong. He never remarried. He held a family together across two continents for fifty years on a laundryman's wages.

Lee Yick Poon's parables
The boy on the balcony
A family sat down to dinner with only a whole fish. When someone flipped it for the other side, a thump came from above — a child had been watching from a balcony, so excited by the abundance that he lost his footing and fell to his death.
昔有一家，圍桌共膳，席間僅一整魚。席間有人將魚翻轉，欲食另一面。忽聞頭頂一聲悶響——原來樓上陽台有孩童窺視，見魚翻轉，以為豐盛，喜不自勝，失足墜落，一命嗚呼。
Let others eat first. Tom Lee: "To this day, I still don't flip the fish."
The fish eyes worth more than pearls
A daughter-in-law treated her father-in-law poorly. At dinner he saved the fish's eyeballs in a jar, saying they were worth as much as pearls. After that she always took the fish head — and the old man got the meaty part.
Cleverness and patience can outmaneuver cruelty without confrontation.
The mother who could not eat
A father took his son to the temple to honor his dead wife. The son said he saw her being pushed away from the offerings. The father hadn't been paying the temple dues. He began paying. The next time, the boy said his mother was eating well.
Honor your obligations to the dead. The living forget; the dead remember.
The ghost of Spofford Alley
His building at 4 Spofford Alley had a reputation. Lillian Lui remembers it as 'old and rickety' — she rolled down the stairs at least twice. Several residents had seen a female ghost, visible only as half a body — either the upper half or the lower half. Those who saw her were said to win a lottery shortly after. Lee Yick Poon always wanted to see the ghost. He never did.
Not every story has a moral. Some are just told in the dark to grandchildren who remember them forever.

The American Chapter
從唐人街到京士頓

From a freight container to a citizenship certificate to a departure date of June 28, 1968 — how the family came to North America.

1929 · Chicago
Lee Yick Poon arrives — as a fake sailor
Gen. 26 · 奕揖 · Poo Lee
Poses as a sailor on a freight container. His father Yau Jee arrived in Chicago around 1897 on the SS Gaelic. They spend twelve years in the same city before Yau Jee dies in 1941. Lee Yick Poon moves to San Francisco in 1942, settles at 4 Spofford Alley. He uses the Chinese Confession Program to legalize his status and is naturalized on 17 February 1965, age 73.
Early 1950s · Oakland
George Lee arrives as a paper son
Gen. 27 · 世錚 · Yin Yang
Lee Yick Poon's third son enters America under a false identity. He opens a grocery store in Alameda. He dies of cancer in July 1984 — his mental strength keeping him alive far longer than doctors projected, determined to stay for his young children. Son: Gordon Lee.
1963 · Hong Kong
The reunion — and the memoir is written
34 years apart
Lee Yick Poon returns during the worst drought in decades. He meets his sons Shai Chun and E Ching — Shai Chun as a grown man; E Ching for the first time ever, as E Ching was born the year his father left. During this visit, Lee Yick Poon writes the diary that Lillian would translate 52 years later.
May 1966 · San Francisco
E Ching's family arrives first
Gen. 27 · 世津 · Lillian's father
Lee Yick Poon's youngest son (born 1929, the year his father left China) arrives with his family. He works from restaurant kitchens to dim sum to market deli; his son Gene sits through every night class at John O'Connell Trade School with him until E Ching earns a janitorial certificate. He retires from San Francisco International Airport. His daughter Lillian translates her grandfather's diary. His daughter Olivia researches the family records.
June 28, 1968 · San Francisco
Shai Chun's family arrives — almost didn't make it
Gen. 27 · 世讓 · Shai Chun
Shai Chun and family arrive on June 28, 1968. Nearly derailed: John K (~18) needed a British passport — almost impossible to obtain. A connection through Gordon's grandmother, linked to the Kowloon Bus Company, secured it at the last minute. The 1967 Hong Kong Leftist Riots had likely forced the sale of the family flat and store at a loss. They crossed the Pacific carrying thirty generations of Lee family history with them.

November 2020 · San Francisco
George Jr. found — after half a century
Gen. 28 · Son of Uncle George
George Lee Jr. — son of Uncle George (尹養) from his first marriage to Janet Louie — had been separated from his father's family since childhood. In November 2020, Maia Lee wrote him a letter. His reply confirmed everything: he remembered the Alameda grocery store on Park Avenue, weekends with his father, the house on 25th Ave and Rivera. Lillian verified with archive photos. George Jr. was driving the 38 Geary Muni line in San Francisco. Gene remembered him as "Mun Jai" — a child riding in Uncle George's Chevy station wagon who one day simply wasn't there anymore. In July 2023, Gilbert reunited with him in person. Lillian wrote: "I know he's smiling now that after all this time, all his kids have found each other." All three of George Sr.'s sons share the initial G — George, Gordon, Gilbert — and now George Jr. too.

The women of the lineage · 家族女性
The mothers who carried the line
c. 1088 CE · Taishan founding
徐氏 · Xu Shi
Wife of Lianjian Weng. Sailed south from Jiangxi in 1102 CE, survived the typhoon that killed her husband, and raised four sons alone. Those sons — 南泰, 南英, 南胜, 南和 — became the founding branches of the entire Taishan Lee clan. Without Xu Shi, there is no family. She is mentioned once in the genealogy and never named again.
b. 1194 CE · Gen. 4
黄氏 · Huang Shi
Wife of Jin Shan (金山, b. 1192 CE), confirmed in the Taishan genealogy page 47. Generation 4 — the woman who with Jin Shan began the specific branch that leads, 26 generations later, to Lee Yick Poon and every Lee in North America today.
601–636 CE · Tang Dynasty
長孫皇后 · Empress Zhangsun
Wife of Emperor Taizong (Li Shimin), your direct ancestor. Widely regarded as the finest empress in Chinese history — a scholar, essayist, and trusted advisor who corrected the emperor's excesses with historical precedent. She wrote a 10-volume work on exemplary women: 女則 (Examples for Women). Taizong said after her death: "I have lost a wonderful help, and I cannot forget her." She died at 36.
died 623 CE · Tang founding
平陽公主 · Princess Pingyang
Sister of Emperor Taizong and daughter of Emperor Gaozu — your direct ancestors' immediate family. When the Tang founding rebellion began, Princess Pingyang raised and commanded her own army independently, securing the northwest. She was the only woman in Chinese history to receive full military funeral honours. Her troops called her "the Army of the Lady."
1868–1917 · Taishan
許瑞錦 · Shew Suey Gum
Wife of Lee Yick Poon, married December 10, 1908. She managed the household and ran embroidery work to support the family while her husband was in America for 34 years. The memoir calls her "very kind, always helping others in need — well loved by everyone in the village." She died in 1917 at 49, never seeing him again after he left. Lee Yick Poon wrote that his only regret was that she left him so early in life.
Gen. 27 · San Francisco
Lillian Wai Yin Lee Lui
Daughter of E Ching Lee, granddaughter of Lee Yick Poon. She translated her grandfather's 1963 diary from classical Chinese — preserving a document that would otherwise have been lost. Without her translation, the generations from Min Ngor through to Yau Jee would be names without stories. This website begins with her work.
