Chapter I
祖源
Ancestral Origins — The Blood Lineage Roots of Our Branch

Shaodian was the common ancestor of the Chinese people's progenitors, the Yan Emperor and the Yellow Emperor — and the founding ancestor of the Li family bloodline. Originally surnamed Gongsun, he was the chieftain of the Youxiong tribe and ruler of the Xiong kingdom during the primitive era, born in 3242 BC and living to 160 years of age.

Shaodian took two daughters of the Qiao clan as consorts. The first consort, Nü Deng, gave birth to the Yan Emperor on the 26th day of the 4th lunar month. The second consort, Fu Bao, gave birth to the Yellow Emperor on March 3, 3274 BC — named Yun, with the courtesy name Xuanyuan.

China has long observed the saying: "On the second day of the second month, the dragon raises its head; on the third day of the third month, Xuanyuan was born."

The Yellow Emperor married Leizu (his principal consort) and had two sons. One son, Xuanxiao (Qingyang), was born by the Ji River and thus took the surname Ji — a surname the Yellow Emperor's legitimate descendants maintained for generations. The Yellow Emperor lived 121 years and ascended the throne in 2697 BC, reigning for 100 years.

Xinzheng in Henan is the birthplace, place of enterprise, and capital of the Chinese civilization's ancestor Xuanyuan the Yellow Emperor. Every year on the 3rd day of the 3rd lunar month, a public ceremony honoring the Yellow Emperor is held at his hometown — a great traditional ceremony of the Chinese people.

"The Yan Emperor and the Yellow Emperor were chieftains of two great tribes. After three battles at Banquan, the Yan Emperor submitted to the Yellow Emperor. Together they formed the Yan-Huang tribal alliance — and the history of the Chinese people thus began." — Li Family Genealogy, Vol. VIII

The Yan Emperor had the head of an ox and horns on his head according to legend, born in the stone cave of Lieshan and raised by the Jiang River. He possessed great virtue, ruled with the power of fire, and was titled the Flame Emperor. Clever from childhood — speaking at three days, walking at five — he taught the people farming, personally tasted hundreds of herbs to discover medicines, made musical instruments, and taught ritual propriety.

The Yan Emperor's mausoleum is located in Yanling, Hunan Province. The Yellow Emperor's hometown shrine in Xinzheng, first built during the Han dynasty, now covers over 70,000 square meters. At its heart stands the Yellow Emperor's Treasure Cauldron — 6.99 meters tall, 4.7 meters in diameter, weighing 24 tons — surrounded by eight companion cauldrons representing Love, Longevity, Wealth, Career, Peace, Abundance, Wisdom, and Governance. The plaza's 13-meter red granite boulevard and 5-meter yellow granite Ancestor Worship Sacred Path symbolize that the descendants of Yan and Huang share connected bloodlines, and that the torch is passed down through the generations.

Chapter II
李姓何时何地何人形成
When, Where, and By Whom Was the Li Surname Formed

Primitive surnames were the clan names of matrilineal tribes — the character for "surname" (姓) is itself composed of "女" (woman) and "生" (born). Many ancient surnames derived from the female side, such as Ji (姬) and Jiang (姜). As descendants multiplied, one lineage divided into several branches scattered to various places, each with a special symbol as its marker — the clan name (氏).

The Evolution of the Li Surname

公孙 Gōngsūn Original surname of Shaodian, founding ancestor of the Chinese nation
姬 Jī Yellow Emperor's surname, derived from the Ji River where he was born
偓 Wò (Yān) Granted surname to Gaotao by Emperor Yao, meaning "Yan"
理 Lǐ Official title surname: Gaotao served as Grand Judge (大理官)
李 Lǐ "Pointing to a tree" — adopted 1122 BC in memory of the plum tree that saved their lives

The Li surname's profound origin story begins with Li Wei, a court official who offered honest remonstrance to the tyrannical last ruler of the Shang dynasty, King Zhou. Executed for his uprightness in 1049 BC at age 51, his wife Lady Qi and He fled with their young son to survive.

With food exhausted, they survived by eating the fruit of a plum tree (李树). To forever remember this grace, and to conceal their true identity from the king's pursuers, Lady Qi combined the characters 木 (tree) and 子 (child/seed) to form 李 — changing their surname from the old 理 to the new 李. The father's name Liwei became Li Zhen (利贞) — the last two characters of the I Ching's Qian hexagram phrase: "元亨利贞" — Yuan Heng Li Zhen.

The character "理" and "李" are homophones. "理" contains the character "田" (field), indicating its connection to the Yellow Emperor's bloodline. "木" and "子" together form "李" — taking the tree's name as their own. This is the origin of the saying "pointing to a tree to take as a surname" (指树为姓).

"利贞 lived to 78 years old (1069–992 BC), and is the 34th generation of the Chinese surname lineage. According to the Study of Surnames: the three characters '理,' '里,' and '李' were interchangeable in antiquity." — Li Family Genealogy

From the founding of the Li surname with Lizhen to today, the Li surname has a history of over 3,079 years.

Chapter III
历代先祖
Ancestors Through the Ages
皋
Gaotao (皋陶) — Grand Judge of Yao
Born 2457 BC · Lived 91 Years
Originally named Tingjian, Gaotao was the chieftain of the Eastern Yi tribe and served as Grand Judge — head of Chinese jurisprudence — under Emperor Yao. He established the five punishments, proclaimed the Five Teachings, Five Rites, and Nine Virtues, and is regarded as the founding ancestor of Chinese law. His descendants took his official title "大理" (Grand Judge) as their surname: 理 (Lǐ).
理
Li Wei (理微) — The Upright Official
c. 1100–1049 BC · Lived 51 Years
Courtesy name Deling, Li Wei served as Earl of Zhongjun at the Shang court. He offered honest remonstrance to the tyrannical King Zhou but was executed for his uprightness. His death forced his family to flee, leading to the transformation of the surname from 理 to 李. The New Book of Tang records him as one "who was upright and would not submit."
贞
Li Zhen (利贞) — Founding Ancestor of the Li Surname
1069–992 BC · 34th Generation from Chinese Nation's Founding
Son of Li Wei, Li Zhen adopted the surname 李 in 1122 BC after surviving on plum tree fruit. He settled in Luyi County, eastern Henan. Named to commemorate both the plum tree's grace and the I Ching's phrase "元亨利贞". He is the first ancestor of the Li (李) surname, now the world's largest surname.
耳
Li Er (李耳) — Laozi, Father of Taoism
17th Generation Descendant of Li Zhen · Spring and Autumn Period
Courtesy name Boyang, known as Laozi (Old Master). Born in Lixiang Quren Village, Ku County (Chu state territory). He served as the Zhou Royal Court's Keeper of the Archives (柱下史) — equivalent to director of the national library. Author of the Tao Te Ching in 5,000 characters — the foundational text of Taoism. Confucius called him a teacher of his generation: "Of birds I know they can fly; of fish I know they can swim... but the dragon, I cannot tell."
昺
Li Yan (李昺) — The Branching Point
8th Generation Descendant of Laozi
Courtesy name Guiyuan, Li Yan served as Grand Censor under the Qin state. His four sons — Chong, Bian, Zhao, and Ju — established the great family branches. The eldest son Chong founded the Long Xi branch (Gansu), which later divided into 39 houses. The fourth son Ju founded the Zhao Commandery branch (Henan), which divided into eastern, southern, and western factions — all becoming prominent local clans.
渊
Li Yuan (李渊) — Emperor Gaozu, Founder of the Tang Dynasty
566–635 AD
Courtesy name Shude, Li Yuan was a Han Chinese from Long Xi, Gansu. His grandfather Li Hu served as Grand Commandant under Western Wei; his father Li Bing served as Grand Censor under Northern Zhou. After the Sui dynasty's collapse amid peasant uprisings, Li Yuan raised his army in 617 AD and captured the capital. In 618 AD he founded the Tang dynasty — China's golden age — which lasted 289 years through 14 emperors. He had 22 sons and is the direct imperial ancestor of the Tang Li royal family.

Emperor Taizong Li Shimin (born 599 AD) reigned for 23 years, died in 683 AD at age 84, and is buried at Zhaoling in Liquan County, Shaanxi. He had 14 sons.

Furthermore, all civil officials and military generals who helped Li Yuan and Li Shimin conquer the realm — or who rendered meritorious service to the Tang dynasty — were granted the imperial surname Li. Those successively given the Li surname included the families of Xu, Xing, An, Du, Hu, Hong, Guo, Ma, Zhang, Cao, Luo, and from ethnic minorities: the Xianyu, Abu, Adao, Sheli, and Zhuye clans — greatly expanding the Li surname population.

The Guangyun records 12 commanderies of the Li clan, and the Tang dynasty's New Collection of Surname Genealogies notes that among all major surnames across key prefectures, the Li clan consistently ranked first.

Chapter V
李氏迁徙
Migration of the Li Clan

The Li surname originated in China's Yellow River basin. After more than 3,000 years, descendants spread from Shaanxi and Zhao Commandery southward across China, and from southern China to all parts of the world.

According to the Li Clan Genealogy, the southward migration during the Tang dynasty occurred mainly three times, followed by further migrations overseas:

I
Early Tang · 668–670 AD
First Southern Migration
Li clan members from Henan followed Chen Zheng and Chen Yuanguang, father and son, into Fujian to open up and establish Zhangzhou — the earliest Li clan people to enter Fujian.
II
An-Shi Rebellion · 756–763 AD
Second Southern Migration
"The Central Plains were in turmoil and the gentry fled southward" — during the devastating An Lushan Rebellion, considerable numbers of Li clan descendants migrated south.
III
Late Tang through Five Dynasties
Third Southern Migration
Due to prolonged turmoil in the Central Plains, Li clan members traveled from Chang'an to Kaifeng then Fujian, or entered directly from Henan following Wang Chao and Wang Shen, settling in Putian, Jinjiang, and beyond.
IV
Late Ming / Early Qing
Migration to Ryukyu
The Ming dynasty granted the Ryukyu Kingdom "thirty-six Fujian families skilled in operating boats" — among them Li clan members. Many Fujianese Li clan members settled in Kumemura near Naha, Okinawa.
V
Post Opium Wars · 19th Century
Migration to Southeast Asia
Able-bodied Li surname young men from Guangdong, Fujian, and Hainan departed through Hong Kong, Macau, Xiamen, and Shantou, sailed southward by ship through great hardships to seek their livelihood in Southeast Asia.
VI
Modern Era
Migration to Europe & Americas
Many more Li surname people crossed the vast oceans to seek their development in European and American countries on both shores of the Atlantic Ocean — the Li name truly becoming global.

The surname originated in the Yellow River basin of China. After more than 3,000 years, descendants have spread from Shaanxi and Zhao Commandery southward across China, and from southern China to all parts of the world. Across the Han, Jin, Tang, and Song dynasties, the Li clan developed into major local clans, with some assimilating into the Yi, Bai, Miao, Yao, Dong, Zhuang, Shui, Naxi, Hui, and other ethnic groups.

During the Wei, Jin, Northern and Southern Dynasties period, the Li clan had already become one of the largest surnames nationwide. Among the Central Plains' prominent clans, the Cui, Lu, Li, and Zheng were known as the four great distinguished families — regarded as the most esteemed gentry of their age.
