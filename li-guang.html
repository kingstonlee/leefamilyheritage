李廣父子
Li Guang and His Sons

Military commanders of the Western Han — archers and frontier guardians against the Xiongnu. Narrative drawn from the Records of the Grand Historian (Shiji), biography of General Li, with modern summary.

Chinese
李广父子 (simplified) · 李廣父子 (traditional)
Role
Famous Han dynasty frontier generals — “Flying General” (飛將軍)
Birth
Unknown — from Longxi Chengji (隴西成紀), present-day Jingning area, Gansu
Death
119 BCE — suicide after becoming lost on campaign

Overview

Li Guang (?–119 BCE) and his sons served the Western Han as frontier commanders. A native of Longxi Chengji (present-day Jingning County, Gansu Province), he rose through posts such as Cavalry Commandant, Commandant of Valiant Cavalry, and Governor of Youbeiping. Celebrated for archery, he defended the northern commanderies against the Xiongnu for decades and was known as the “Flying General.”

Li Guang first joined the army against the Xiongnu in the fourteenth year of Emperor Wen (166 BCE). Under Emperor Jing he fought in the suppression of the Rebellion of the Seven States but received no reward because he had privately accepted a general’s seal from the Prince of Liang. Under Emperor Wu, as Governor of Youbeiping, the Xiongnu reportedly avoided the region for years. In 119 BCE, campaigning with Wei Qing, he became separated from the main force, missed the decisive engagement, and took his own life rather than face trial.

His son Li Gan earned noble rank for military merit but was later shot by Huo Qubing after wounding Wei Qing. His grandson Li Ling, with five thousand infantry, was surrounded by a vastly larger Xiongnu force; after defeat and surrender, his clan was destroyed by imperial order.

Battle of Mayi and literary allusions

In 133 BCE the Han used the city of Mayi (present-day Shuoxian, Shanxi) as bait to lure the Xiongnu Chanyu within the frontier, with ambushing troops hidden in the valleys. Li Guang served as Cavalry General under Han Anguo; the Chanyu detected the trap and withdrew.

Li Guang never gained marquis rank despite decades of service — a fate proverbially paired with the line about Feng Tang: “Feng Tang grows old easily; Li Guang finds it hard to be enfeoffed.”

Life and career

Ancestry and youth

Li Guang’s ancestor was Li Xin, a Qin general who had defeated the Yan crown prince Dan. Li Guang mastered the family archery tradition and became an extraordinary bowman.

In 166 BCE, when the Xiongnu raided the border, Li Guang enlisted. His skill brought promotion to Gentleman of the Palace and mounted imperial escort. Emperor Wen reportedly sighed that had Li Guang lived in Gaozu’s founding wars, a ten-thousand-household marquisate would have come easily.

Seven States and early frontier posts

Under Emperor Jing he became Commandant of Longxi and Chief of the Mounted Gentlemen. In the Seven States revolt he served under Zhou Yafu, capturing an enemy banner at Changyi — yet because he accepted a seal privately from the King of Liang, the court gave him no reward afterward.

He held governor posts including Shanggu, Shangjun, Longxi, Beidi, Yanmen, Dai, and Yunzhong, repeatedly clashing with the Xiongnu. Gongsun Kunye warned the throne that Li Guang’s daring risked his life; transfers followed.

The hundred horsemen

When a court eunuch’s escort was destroyed by three Xiongnu eagle hunters, Li Guang judged their tactics, took a hundred horsemen, killed two and captured one — then saw several thousand Xiongnu appear. His men wished to flee; he refused:

We are dozens of li from our main force. If we flee, they will ride us down. If we stay, they will think us a decoy and dare not strike.

He advanced to within two li of the enemy, ordered every man to dismount and remove saddles — then killed a Xiongnu officer on a white horse who rode out to supervise. At nightfall the Xiongnu, fearing ambush, withdrew; at dawn Li Guang rejoined the army.

Emperor Wu, Cheng Bushi, and Mayi

After Wu’s accession (141 BCE), ministers praised Li Guang’s courage; he became Commandant of the Weiyang Palace Guard while Cheng Bushi guarded Changle. Both had commanded frontier armies, yet their styles opposed: Li Guang’s administration was simple — loose formations, camps by water and grass, minimal night watches and paperwork, but scouts far forward so disaster rarely struck. Cheng Bushi enforced ranks, watches, and documents all night; soldiers were safer but exhausted, and preferred serving under Li Guang. Li Guang’s role in the Mayi ambush is summarized in the section above.

Capture and ransom

Four years after the Mayi episode, Li Guang led his army through Yanmen Pass. A Xiongnu force roughly twice his size surrounded him. Badly outnumbered and wounded, he was eventually captured. The Chanyu had long admired Li Guang and ordered: “Capture Li Guang alive!” The enemy placed the wounded general in a rope sling between two horses. After more than ten li, Li Guang feigned death; seeing a young Xiongnu riding a fine horse beside him, he sprang up, threw the youth down, seized bow and arrows, whipped the horse south, shot at pursuers, and escaped — then gathered survivors and returned to the capital. The court ruled his losses merited execution; he ransomed his life with cash and became a commoner.

Baling Pavilion and return to Youbeiping

During retirement Li Guang rode out at night with one attendant; returning past Baling Pavilion, a drunk captain barred the way. The attendant said, “This is the former General Li.” The captain replied, “Even a current general may not pass at night — let alone a former one!” and detained them until morning. Soon after, Xiongnu pressure brought Li Guang back as Governor of Youbeiping; he took the captain into service and executed him, then memorialized his fault. Emperor Wu answered at length from military classics — essentially excusing the revenge — a scene readers have long cited as showing both imperial indulgence and Li Guang’s harsh pride.

At Youbeiping the Xiongnu called him Han’s “Flying General” and avoided his district for years.

Tiger and rock

Hunting, he mistook a stone for a tiger and drove an arrow so deep it could not be withdrawn; a second shot failed to penetrate — one of the most famous vignettes Sima Qian uses to convey his strength.

Character

He shared rewards with rank-and-file, ate simple rations, and for decades drew a two-thousand-shi salary yet left little estate. Demanding deadly accuracy, he often fought at such close range that he was wounded — yet men followed him willingly.

Campaigns under Emperor Wu

In 123 BCE he followed Wei Qing from Dingxiang with little profit while others won marquisates. In 121 BCE, with Zhang Qian, four thousand men met forty thousand Xiongnu; his son Li Gan rode through the ring of enemies to steady morale. Nearly annihilated, they were saved when Zhang Qian arrived — Li Guang went unrewarded.

Never enfeoffed

Cousins and subordinates gained marquis titles while Li Guang did not. A diviner suggested his worst offense was deceiving and slaughtering surrendered Qiang — “no crime greater than killing those who have already surrendered.”

119 BCE — the northern campaign and death

In the fourth year of Yuanshou, Wei Qing and Huo Qubing drove deep into the desert. Li Guang begged for active duty; aged and judged unlucky by the emperor, he was finally named Vanguard General — yet Wei Qing held secret instructions not to let him face the Chanyu, hoping instead to win merit for Gongsun Ao. Li Guang pleaded to lead the van: “My unit is Vanguard — yet I am sent on the eastern road, long and waterless. I have fought the Xiongnu since I bound my hair; now let me die facing the Chanyu!” Wei Qing refused; a chief clerk delivered written orders; Li Guang left without farewell, raging.

With Zhao Shiqi he took the eastern route, lost his guide, missed the rendezvous, and met Wei Qing only on the withdrawal. Wei Qing’s clerks pressed for depositions; Li Guang took responsibility: “The colonels are innocent — I lost the way.” Back at headquarters he told his men:

Since I bound my hair Guang has fought the Xiongnu more than seventy times. Now that I finally followed the Grand General against the Chanyu, he diverted me to a distant road where we lost our way — is this not Heaven’s will? And I am over sixty; I cannot again face clerks with brush and knife.

He drew his sword and cut his throat. “Whether they knew him or not, old or young, the people wept” — Sima Qian’s epitaph for the Flying General.

Li Gan, Li Ling, and Sima Qian

Li Gan, seeking revenge for his father’s death, wounded Wei Qing but was shot by Huo Qubing; Emperor Wu’s favor toward Huo shielded the killer — an injustice later readers paired with Sima Qian’s outrage.

Li Ling was posthumous son of Li Guang’s eldest son Li Danghu. In autumn of the second Tianhan year (99 BCE), while Li Guangli attacked with the main cavalry, Li Ling led five thousand infantry from Juyan to fix the Chanyu’s attention. Surrounded by tens of thousands of steppe horse-archers, his men exhausted their arrows and killed vast numbers before Li Ling surrendered — “I have no face to report to His Majesty.” Relief failed to arrive; Lu Bode hung back in shame; Li Guangli retreated without decisive battle. Emperor Wu exterminated Li Ling’s kin.

Sima Qian, believing Li Ling maligned and Li Guangli’s defeat overlooked, spoke for him — and was castrated. Modern scholars suggest that suffering biased Sima Qian toward Li Guang’s family, lending the “Biography of General Li” heightened literary pathos as well as history.

Sons (summary)

Li Guang had three sons: Li Danghu (died early; posthumous son Li Ling), Li Jiao (Governor of Dai, predeceased his father), and Li Gan, who campaigned with Huo Qubing, seized enemy banners, was enfeoffed, and succeeded to his father’s palace post — before the fatal clash with Huo Qubing over Wei Qing.

Source tradition: Sima Qian, Shiji (“Biography of General Li” 李將軍列傳). This page is a narrative summary for family reading, not a critical edition.
