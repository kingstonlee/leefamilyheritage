涼武昭王 · 李暠
Li Hao — Martial & Luminous Prince of Liang

Founder of Western Liang (西涼) in the Hexi corridor; named in the Tang imperial pedigree as posthumous Emperor Xingsheng (興聖皇帝). His life is told in the standard-history biography commissioned under Emperor Taizong of Tang (唐太宗) and completed in 648 CE — a court history compiled by another man in the same imperial lineage who looked back on him as forefather.

Names
諱暠 Hào · courtesy 玄盛 Xuansheng · childhood name 長生 Changsheng
Origin
隴西成紀 Longxi Chengji — same homeland tradition as Han general Li Guang
Lifespan
351–417 CE (died aged 67, per Jin Shu)
Titles & temple
Ruler as Duke / Gong of Liang; posthumous Wuzhao Wang (武昭王); temple name Taizu (太祖); tomb name 建世陵
Polity
Western Liang — held power in the western river region (河西) about twenty-four years from enthronement (晋安帝隆安四年) to fall under Song (宋少帝景平元年), per the historian’s closing reckoning

Why this biography matters

The account is not folklore or a later novelization. It is the primary historiographic portrait of Li Hao in the official Jin Shu, presented by Fang Xuanling (房玄齡) and colleagues by order of Tang Taizong. That makes it one of the oldest sustained narratives we possess about this ancestor — written roughly 1,400 years ago, within living institutional memory of the Western Liang, and preserved ever since in the standard histories.

Ancestry and youth

From Li Guang to the western gentry

The opening lines answer the question of how the Longxi Li connect to the Han hero Li Guang: Li Hao was the sixteenth-generation descendant of Guang, the former general of the Han (漢前將軍廣之十六世孫). The text then strings named ancestors:

Li Zhongxiang (仲翔) — early Han general; fought rebellious Qiang at Suchang (near Didao); outnumbered, he died there.

Li Bokao (伯考) — went to mourn, buried his father east of Didao, and stayed; the family became for generations a prominent house of the western regions (世為西州右姓): established commandery-level gentry along the Longxi–Hexi corridor.

Jin and Former Liang generations

The biography names four closer generations who anchor the family in the late Three Kingdoms and Western Jin transition — when the northwest was tied first to Wei’s administration and then to Jin and to regional regimes such as Former Liang:

Li Yong (雍) — gaozu 高祖; Governor of Dongguan (東莞太守) under Jin.

Li Rou (柔) — zengzu 曾祖; Governor of Beidi (北地太守) under Jin.

Li Yan (弇) — zu 祖; served Zhang Gui (張軌), founder of Former Liang, as General of the Martial Guards and Marquis of Anshi pavilion (武衛將軍、安世亭侯).

Li Chang (昶) — father; admired from youth but died young; Li Hao was a posthumous son (遺腹子).

Together, this passage confirms that Li Hao’s forebears were not marginal farmers but serial office-holding families in the northwest — the milieu in which Hexi politics unfolded between Wei, Jin, and the Zhang-led order at Liangzhou.

Family genealogy note. Our consolidated pedigree (including the Taishan 2018 documentation on the main site) correlates intermediate ancestors such as 李楼 (b. 255 CE) and 李发 with this era. The Jin Shu does not spell out every generation between Li Guang and Li Hao; treat Yong and Rou as the documentary anchor for governors of that period in this line, and map named descendants in private registers to them only where your charts already justify it.

In temperament the historian credits Li Hao with scholarship, breadth of mind, skill in letters, later study of Sunzi and Wu Qi, and humane government — a portrait of a ruler who grounded power in learning and ritual as much as in arms.

The prophecy of the white-foreheaded colt

While lodging with Lu Guang’s Grand Astrologer Guo Na (郭黁) and Guo’s maternal younger brother Song Yao (宋繇), Li Hao heard Guo tell Song that Song would rise to the highest ministerial rank, while “this Li gentleman” had the portion of a realm (國土之分) — and that in Song’s household a gray horse had foaled a white-foreheaded colt, marking the hour. Years later, when Li Hao hung back from taking Dunhuang, Song returned from service under Duan Ye and said: Have you forgotten Guo Na’s words? The white-foreheaded colt has already been born. Li Hao then accepted leadership. The episode ties political destiny in the text to a concrete, remembered omen.

Rise in Dunhuang

Near the end of Lu Guang’s power, Duan Ye styled himself governor of Liangzhou. Li Hao first served as magistrate of Xiaogu; after officials recommended him for warmth and firm benevolent rule, he became governor of Dunhuang. He hesitated until Song Yao — who had heard the omen years before in Guo Na’s company — came home to Dunhuang and repeated Guo Na’s prediction about the foal (see above). Hao accepted, took higher military titles, and acknowledged Duan Ye as overlord.

Break with Suo Si and expansion

When Duan Ye declared himself king of Liang, Suo Si (索嗣) slandered Li Hao and was sent to replace him at Dunhuang. Advisors including Song Yao urged resistance; Hao’s sons Shiye (士業 — later personal name of heir Li Xin) and Rang (讓) joined the fight. Suo Si was defeated and fled toward Zhangye. Though once sworn friends, Hao resented the betrayal and pressed charges; Duan Ye executed Suo Si and enlarged Li Hao’s authority westward.

In the fourth year of Long’an, Tang Yao (唐瑤) circulated a proclamation among six commanderies elevating Li Hao as grand commander, supreme general, Duke of Liang, and governor of Qin and Liang circuits. Hao declared amnesty, adopted the era name Gengzi (庚子), then honored ancestors posthumously and organized civil and military appointments — the formal founding gesture of Western Liang.

Allegiance to Jin

Though autonomous, Li Hao framed his regime as loyal to the Eastern Jin: lengthy memorials (carried secretly by envoys such as Huang Shi and the monk Faquan) compare his situation to Dou Rong (竇融) supporting the Han heartland from the northwest. In the first year of Yixi he changed the era name to Jianchu (建初) and continued to justify local reign titles because imperial calendars could not reach the frontier.

Capital at Jiuquan and letter to his sons

Facing Juqu Mengxun (北涼), Li Hao chose to move his seat eastward to Jiuquan while leaving Song Yao and son Rang to hold Dunhuang — the kingdom’s link to the Western Regions. Before and after that move the biography preserves his own words to his children: on integrity, fear of pride, diligence in judgment, and reading history. These passages have survived for some sixteen centuries as part of the official record.

After noting that he never sought mere worldly profit and that office had come to him by circumstance, he warns that burden and family honor weigh heavily — and that preventing trouble early and reading human character cannot be reduced to commands:

I have always conducted myself with integrity, never seeking worldly gains… Today’s course is not what I originally wished for — yet circumstance has driven me to bear this territory. Though I have weighed human affairs, I still do not know Heaven’s will.

His concrete ethics — still startlingly immediate in translation — include restraint with wine and speech, examining anger and joy, loving without blindness and hating without denying good in others, judging fairly without letting kin escape punishment, and refusing to surround oneself with flatterers:

Moderate your drinking, be cautious in speech. When joyful or angry, always think carefully. Love but know the evil; hate yet recognize the good… It is extremely difficult to remain humble in wealth and honor. Keep this principle deeply in your heart, never forgetting it for a moment.
Do not forget the lessons of past successes and failures. In your leisure time after court, reflect on classical texts. To stand facing a wall without learning is to be no true person.

The historian also quotes the dense classical precepts that follow in the same vein (personnel, punishments, listening to lawsuits). In the original:

節酒慎言，喜怒必思……從善如順流，去惡如探湯。

Theme: mindful habits, fair rewards and punishments, counsel from many voices, and moral self-command — preserved in 《晉書》 sections corresponding to the biography’s sequential paragraphs on his testament.

Culture and character

At Dunhuang he stored jade seals obtained from Khotan, built Jinggong Hall (靖恭之堂) by the water south of the gate for deliberation and drills, displayed portraits of sage kings, loyal ministers, filial sons, and heroic exemplars, enlarged schools (including hundreds of students at the academy), and composed or commissioned fu such as Shuzhi fu (述志賦), Huai shu fu (槐樹賦), and Da jiu rong fu (大酒容賦). He quoted Zhuge Liang’s family admonitions to urge his sons toward duty and learning. The surviving description aligns with a ruler who believed legitimacy rested on education and moral example as much as on armies.

Death and succession

Ill, Li Hao told Song Yao that death itself did not grieve him — what pained him was that his great aims remained unfulfilled. He charged Song to steady the young heir and not let strategy lose its moral center. He died in the thirteenth year of his reign, aged sixty-seven. His eldest heir apparent had died young; Li Xin (歆), courtesy Shiye, succeeded. The same chapter continues with Li Xin’s reign, rivalry with Mengxun, defeat at places such as Liaoquan, death in battle, and the eventual flight of kin — including Li Chonger (重耳) to the south and later service under Wei — closing the Western Liang story in the standard history.

Personal resonance is for each reader; but the biography’s closing emphasis on duty uncompleted at sixty-seven invites comparison with later generations in this line who measured their own lives against whether “the work” was finished — a thread modern descendants may weigh privately against letters such as Lee Yick Poon’s.
