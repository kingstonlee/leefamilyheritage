涼武昭王 · 李暠
Li Hao — Martial & Luminous Prince of Liang

Founder of Western Liang (西涼) in the Hexi corridor; named in the Tang imperial pedigree as posthumous Emperor Xingsheng (興聖皇帝). Summary below follows the standard-history biography in the Jin Shu.

Names
諱暠 Hào · courtesy 玄盛 Xuansheng · childhood name 長生 Changsheng
Origin
隴西成紀 Longxi Chengji — same homeland tradition as Han general Li Guang
Lifespan
351–417 CE (died aged 67, per Jin Shu)
Titles & temple
Ruler as Duke / Gong of Liang; posthumous Wuzhao Wang (武昭王); temple name Taizu (太祖); tomb name 建世陵
Polity
Western Liang — held power in the western river region (河西) about twenty-four years from enthronement (晋安帝隆安四年) to fall under Song (宋少帝景平元年), per the historian’s closing reckoning

Ancestry and youth

The biography traces the Li of Longxi to Li Guang (漢前將軍廣), calling Li Hao his sixteenth-generation descendant. Earlier generations served the Han and Jin; his grandfather Li Yan (弇) served Zhang Gui of Former Liang as General of the Martial Guards and Marquis of Anshi. His father Li Chang (昶) died young; Hao was born posthumously. The text describes him as fond of learning, calm and astute, well read in classics and histories, and later trained in arms and Sun Wu’s military writings.

Rise in Dunhuang

Near the end of Lu Guang’s power, Duan Ye styled himself governor of Liangzhou. Li Hao first served as magistrate of Xiaogu; after officials recommended him for warmth and firm benevolent rule, he became governor of Dunhuang. He hesitated until his maternal cousin Song Yao (宋繇) reminded him of an omen once spoken by Lu Guang’s astrologer Guo Na (郭黁): the “white-foreheaded colt” had been born — time to act. Hao accepted, took higher military titles, and acknowledged Duan Ye as overlord.

Break with Suo Si and expansion

When Duan Ye declared himself king of Liang, Suo Si (索嗣) slandered Li Hao and was sent to replace him at Dunhuang. Advisors including Song Yao urged resistance; Hao’s sons Shiye (士業 — later personal name of heir Li Xin) and Rang (讓) joined the fight. Suo Si was defeated and fled toward Zhangye. Though once sworn friends, Hao resented the betrayal and pressed charges; Duan Ye executed Suo Si and enlarged Li Hao’s authority westward.

In the fourth year of Long’an, Tang Yao (唐瑤) circulated a proclamation among six commanderies elevating Li Hao as grand commander, supreme general, Duke of Liang, and governor of Qin and Liang circuits. Hao declared amnesty, adopted the era name Gengzi (庚子), then honored ancestors posthumously and organized civil and military appointments — the formal founding gesture of Western Liang.

Allegiance to Jin

Though autonomous, Li Hao framed his regime as loyal to the Eastern Jin: lengthy memorials (carried secretly by envoys such as Huang Shi and the monk Faquan) compare his situation to Dou Rong (竇融) supporting the Han heartland from the northwest. In the first year of Yixi he changed the era name to Jianchu (建初) and continued to justify local reign titles because imperial calendars could not reach the frontier.

Capital at Jiuquan and testament

Facing Juqu Mengxun (北涼), Li Hao chose to move his seat eastward to Jiuquan while leaving Song Yao and son Rang to hold Dunhuang — the kingdom’s link to the Western Regions. He issued a handwritten admonition to his sons on moderation, justice, listening to the people, and studying failure — material the historian preserves at length.

節酒慎言，喜怒必思……從善如順流，去惡如探湯。

Condensed from his counsel to sons in the biography: mindful drinking and speech; rewards and punishments without partiality; seek advice; shun flatterers.

Culture

At Dunhuang he stored jade seals obtained from Khotan, built Jinggong Hall (靖恭之堂) by the water south of the gate for deliberation and drills, displayed portraits of exemplars, enlarged schools, and composed or commissioned fu such as Shuzhi fu (述志賦), Huai shu fu (槐樹賦), and Da jiu rong fu (大酒容賦). He cited Zhuge Liang’s family instructions to encourage his sons.

Death and succession

Ill, Li Hao entrusted Song Yao with guiding the heir, warned against arrogance, and died in the thirteenth year of his reign (age sixty-seven). His eldest heir apparent had died young; Li Xin (歆), courtesy Shiye, succeeded. The same chapter continues with Li Xin’s reign, rivalry with Mengxun, defeat at places such as Liaoquan, death in battle, and the eventual flight of kin — including Li Chonger (重耳) to the south and later service under Wei — closing the Western Liang story in the standard history.
