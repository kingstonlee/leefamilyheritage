### Page 20 Key Reading (Wing Choeng's Entry)

The entry reads left to right in columns:
- **Column 1: 联翁** — title (the venerable Lian)
- **Column 2: 名慧签** — given name: Wai Chim | wife: 朱氏 Lady Zhu
- **Column 3: 讳荣昌** — formal taboo name: **Wing Choeng** (荣昌) | wife: 陈氏 Lady Chen
- **Column 4: 号松年** — literary name: Song Lin (Pine Year)

The 讳 (huì) is the formal name used in ancestor veneration — this is why the family preserved 荣昌/Wing Choeng across 900 years.

## The Four Parables of Lee Yick Poon

1. **The boy on the balcony** — A family's only dish at dinner was a whole fish. When someone flipped it, a thump came from above — a child watching from the balcony, so excited by the abundance that he lost his footing and fell to his death. (Tom Lee still doesn't flip fish.)

2. **Fish eyes worth pearls** — Siu Yip (Gen. 20) was widening a road into Doe Moon when he discovered a coffin full of silver coins. He used the money to buy rice fields yielding 3,000–4,000 dom. People who saw the coins said they looked like fish eyes — they didn't realize each one was worth a string of pearls.

3. **The mother who couldn't eat** — When Lee Yick Poon's wife managed their meager resources, she would serve the children first and pretend to have already eaten. Only later did he understand she had gone without.

4. **The ghost of Spofford Alley** — His building at 4 Spofford Alley had a reputation. Lillian Lui remembers it as "old and rickety" — she rolled down the stairs at least twice. Several residents had seen a female ghost, visible only as half a body. Those who saw her were said to win a lottery shortly after. Lee Yick Poon always wanted to see the ghost. He never did.

## The Chinese Naming System (Reference)

Chinese men of status typically had multiple names used in different contexts:

| Term | Usage | Example (Wing Choeng) |
|------|-------|----------------------|
| 名 (míng) | Primary/given name; used by family | 慧签 Wai Chim |
| 讳 (huì) | Formal taboo name; used in ancestor veneration, genealogies, tombstones | 荣昌 Wing Choeng |
| 字 (zì) | Courtesy name given at adulthood; used by peers | varies per ancestor |
| 号 (hào) | Literary/pseudonym; self-chosen | 松年 Song Lin (Pine Year) |
| 谥 (shì) | Posthumous honorific; granted after death by community or state | 文庄 Wen Zhuang (Qiao Mu) |
| 翁 (wēng) | Honorific suffix meaning "venerable elder"; not part of the name itself | 联翁 (the venerable Lian) |
| 排行 | Generational character shared by all brothers | 永 (Wing) — Gen. 28 brothers |

**Why the family preserved 讳 荣昌 as "Wing Choeng":** The 讳 is specifically the name used in ancestor veneration ceremonies, tomb inscriptions, and genealogical records. It was designed to be remembered across generations for exactly this purpose.
