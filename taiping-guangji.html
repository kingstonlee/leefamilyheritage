Taiping Guangji — selected Longxi Li passages

The Taiping Guangji (太平廣記), compiled in the Northern Song (977–984), gathers Tang and earlier anecdote, Buddhist tale, and zhiguai fiction. It is not a genealogy — yet dozens of entries open with 隴西李, the same honorific surname cluster our Taishan pedigree claims. Read beside the legend cards on the main site (especially 李世民 and 李晟), these texts sketch the cultural world Tang readers associated with that label: ministers, soldiers, poets, and ghosts.

Fiction vs family tree. Marvel tales are not courtroom proof. Use them with standard histories (《舊唐書》《新唐書》《晉書》, etc.) when assessing real kinship.

Family commentary · significance for our line

The following synthesizes discussion among family researchers (2026) after surveying Longxi Li entries in this anthology:

  • 李積 — Already excerpted on our Yiyan / Ji page: great-grandnephew of 酒泉公義琰, signs correspondence only as “Longxi Li Ji.” The 酒泉 title echoes the Hexi corridor where Western Liang under Li Hao flourished; Ji’s generation sits only decades before Li Sheng (李晟, b. 727 CE), our documented Tang general — same elite surname milieu, not a proven twig on our chart.
  • 李義琰 — Zhenguan-era official; Taizong’s reign is the institutional backdrop for the imperial pedigree that eventually reaches Li Sheng’s collateral branch.
  • 李公佐 / 李朝威 — Longxi Li as compilers and narrative voices behind famous tales (e.g. Liu Yi, Wuzhiqi frame).
  • 李賀 — Celebrated poet, labeled grandson of a Tang prince; taboo tragedy over the jinshi; imperial collateral “cousinhood” in the broad sense only.
  • 李舟 / 李徵 / 李翺 — Friendship across rank, metamorphosis fiction later reused by Nakajima Atsushi, and Confucian philosophy beside Han Yu — facets of Longxi Li as literary type.
Composite picture. Taken together, the entries suggest how Tang society imagined Longxi Li: anxious about clan rank (駝李), abundant in office and verse, willing to hang miracle plots on real ministers’ kin, and still prefacing names with 隴西 centuries after dispersal.

“The camel story captures it perfectly: anxious enough about standing to ride through the night on a braying camel — distinguished enough that the story was still being told five hundred years later.” — family note

Further entries

Many other 《太平廣記》 items use anonymous “隴西李生” or minor figures (李益 / 霍小玉傳, 李復言, cautionary drowning or snake tales). They add atmosphere but are not singled out here unless we later document a named match to the Taishan genealogy.
