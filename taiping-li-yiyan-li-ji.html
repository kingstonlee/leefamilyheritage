李義琰 · 李積
Chancellor-class Longxi Li in tale & anecdote

Who they are

李義琰 (Li Yiyan, d. 688) served under Gaozong as vice director of the Chancellery and was part of the Tang elite memorialized in standard histories. The 《太平廣記》 entry preserves a Buddhist zhiguai from 《法苑珠林》: while county magistrate in Huazhou during the Zhenguan era, he investigates a disappearance; at midnight the victim’s ghost appears and reveals the well where the body lies.

唐隴西李義琰，貞觀年中，為華州縣尉。此縣忽失一人，莫知所在。其父兄疑一讎家所害。詣縣陳情。義琰案之，不能得決，夜中執燭，委細窮問。至夜，義琰據案俛首。不覺死人即至。猶帶被傷之狀，云：「某乙打殺，置於某所井中，公可早驗，不然，恐被移向他處，不可尋覓。」義琰即親往，果如所陳。而讎家始具款伏。當聞見者，莫不驚歎。出《法苑珠林》

Tang Longxi Li Yiyan, during the Zhenguan period, served as a county magistrate of Huazhou. A person suddenly went missing in this county, and no one knew where he was. His father and elder brother suspected that an enemy had harmed him. They went to the county office to present their case. Yiyan investigated the matter, but could not reach a conclusion; at midnight with a candle in hand, he carefully and thoroughly questioned everyone. Late into the night, Yiyan leaned over his desk, bowing his head. Unbeknownst to him, the dead man suddenly appeared. He still bore the marks of his injuries and said, “Yi Mou killed me and placed my body in a well at a certain location. Sir, you should inspect it soon; otherwise, I fear it may be moved elsewhere and become impossible to find.” Yiyan immediately went in person, and indeed found things as described. And the enemy finally confessed fully. Those who heard and witnessed this could not help but be astonished and impressed. From Fayuan Zhulin.

Historical anchor. Li Yiyan appears in 《舊唐書》卷八十一 / 《新唐書》列傳 as a moral, cautious minister — not merely fiction. The ghost scene belongs to medieval Buddhist literature illustrating “spirits do not lie,” not to courtroom archives.
Family reading · Taizong’s reign. The anecdote is set in the Zhenguan years (627–649), under Emperor Taizong (李世民) — the same sovereign generation memorialized on our main lineage page as apex of the Tang house from which Li Sheng (李晟, b. 727) descends through a collateral branch. Yiyan is therefore a literary portrait of a Longxi Li official serving exactly when the institutional Tang world Li Sheng later defended was being forged.

李積 — great-grandnephew on record

A separate 《太平廣記》 snippet (《國史補》) ties a later Longxi Li to Yiyan’s lineage by kinship term:

李積。酒泉公義琰姪孫，門戶第一而有清名。常以爵位不如族望，官至司封郎中懷州刺史，與人書札，唯稱隴西李積。出《國史補》

Li Ji was the great-grandnephew of Duke of Jiuquan Yiyan. Though his household ranked first locally and he bore a reputation for integrity, he often considered official rank less important than clan prestige. He rose to Sifeng Langzhong and prefect of Huai Zhou, but in letters to others he signed himself only as “Li Ji of Longxi.” From Guoshi Bu (Supplement to the National History).

This second passage is especially vivid for our site: it states outright that 門族 prestige could outweigh office — the same Longxi Li identity emphasized from Li Guang through the Tang imperial pedigree.

酒泉公 · Jiuquan · Li Ji. The text calls Yiyan 酒泉公 (Duke / lord associated with Jiuquan). That place-name sits in the Hexi corridor — the same broad western arena where Li Hao’s Western Liang held power. Strict genealogy: Li Ji is Yiyan’s documented great-grandnephew, not by this snippet alone a descendant of Li Hao in the male line. Family interpretation: the title still resonates geographically and culturally with the Western Liang story our site treats as flagship history, and Ji’s career falls within living memory only a few generations before General Li Sheng — collateral glitter in the same Tang Longxi Li galaxy we claim by pedigree rhetoric.
