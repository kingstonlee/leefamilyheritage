隴西李 · 唐人的幾張側影
Poets, scholars, tale collectors — same surname world as Li Sheng

These notes summarize how 《太平廣記》 and related Tang texts label famous men as 隴西李. They enrich the cultural setting around our confirmed ancestor 李晟 (Li Sheng, 727–793 CE), general and stabilizer of the mid-Tang throne — see the legend card on the main page. They do not, by themselves, prove collateral cousinship on paper.

Family reading · 2026. Reading many “Longxi Li” entries together suggests what it meant to carry that name in Li Sheng’s century: commanders and ministers, poets and philosophers, compilers of marvel tales — and cautionary ghosts. Prefacing one’s name with 隴西李 remained a statement of pedigree long after branches had scattered.

李公佐 · Li Gongzuo

隴西李公佐 appears as the scholar-official who hears Nun Miaoji’s riddle in 《續幽怪錄》, traces the word-puzzle to the killers’ names, and meets her again years later — a classic “detective of the bizarre” persona. In 《戎幕閑談》, framed through Yang Heng’s reminiscence, he discusses Prefect 李湯’s Huai River chain-monster episode and later discovers the archaic 《岳瀆經》 passage about 無支祁 (Wuzhiqi), aligning folklore with a forged-antique text tradition.

Gongzuo’s appearances knit together travel, anomaly, and exegesis — the intellectual habits of a Tang literatus who happened to be filed under Longxi Li.

李朝威 · Li Chaowei & the Liu Yi letter

The 《柳毅》 entry concludes with the compiler’s flourish: 「隴西李朝威敍而嘆曰」 — “Li Chaowei of Longxi narrated and sighed,” attributing to him the celebrated dragon-daughter romance (scholar ferries a displaced goddess’s plea to her father at Dongting Lake). Whether read as authorial signature or fictive frame, it marks another Longxi Li as voice behind the fiction, alongside Gongzuo.

李賀 · Li He (791–817)

The anthology quotes 《宣室志》: 隴西李賀字長吉, identified as a grandson of Tang Prince Zheng — 唐鄭王之孫. His father’s name (晋肅) barred him from the jinshi kit because of taboo homophony with 進 in 進士 — a bureaucratic cruelty the tale underscores as vividly as any historian could. Li He’s compressed, spectral verse made him one of the most distinctive Tang poets; here he is framed as imperial collateral, the same broad Tang kin universe from which Li Sheng’s line descends.

李舟 · Li Zhou

隴西李舟, in 《摭言》, writes to minister Qi Ying while himself remaining a commoner — friendship justified by character, not rank. The letter sketches illness, farming with sons, estrangement from court fashion, and tender concern for an exalted friend’s health. For family narrative it is a rare glimpse of a Longxi Li who refused to perform obsequious distance across a vast social gap.

李徵 · Li Zheng becomes a tiger

隴西李徵, 《宣室志》: a brilliant examination graduate and imperial-clan man whose pride breaks against low office; madness, flight into the hills, and transformation into a tiger; then a roadside dialogue in human voice with his old acquaintance 袁傪 (Yuan Can — imperial censor on mission; the text reads 陳郡袁傪), entrusting verses and family to him.

Secondary literature sometimes blurs Yuan Can with other Tang writers; the 《太平廣記》 passage itself names 袁傪.

Nakajima Atsushi’s Sangetsuki (山月記) famously remakes this metamorphosis — a Longxi Li nightmare that crossed into modern Japanese letters and from there into world readership.

李翺 · Li Ao

The 《太平廣記》 thread on Chang Gun and Ouyang Zhan mentions 隴西李翺 as author of Ouyang’s biography — the Li Ao who studied with Han Yu and helped steer mid-Tang Confucian rhetoric toward later Neo-Confucian concerns. A single sentence in the anthology, but it ties the Longxi Li label to moral philosophy, not only to romance or ghosts.

Sources vary by tale (《宣室志》《摭言》《異聞集》《續幽怪錄》《戎幕閑談》…); all are mediated through 《太平廣記》 or its citation chains. Cross-check Chinese Text Project.
