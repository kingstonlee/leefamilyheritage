李氏 · 駝李
Longxi Li racing the “four surnames” list

Relevance to our line

This is not about one named ancestor on our chart; it is about why “隴西李氏” mattered politically. The story survives in 《太平廣記》卷一百八十四 · 《貢舉七》 · 李氏, citing 《朝野僉載》. It explains the folk epithet 駝李 (Camel Li): anxiety over clan ranking under Emperor Xiaowen of Northern Wei.

後魏孝文帝定四姓，隴西李氏大姓，恐不入，星夜乘鳴駝，倍程至洛。時四姓已定訖。故至今謂之駝李焉。出《朝野僉載》

The Later Wei Emperor Xiaowen established the four prominent clans. The Li clan of Longxi was a major family, but fearing they would not be included, they traveled day and night on a braying camel, doubling their pace to reach Luoyang. By then the four clans had already been finalized. Therefore to this day they are called Tu Li (“Camel Li”). From Chaoye Qianzai.

Reading for descendants. Medieval elites competed for recognition as “great surname” (大姓). Our site’s deep pedigree (Li Guang → Li Hao → Tang house) belongs inside that same cultural prestige economy — even though this anecdote is gossip, not proof of any one branch’s inclusion in Xiaowen’s list.
